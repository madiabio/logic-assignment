\section{Discussion}

The three-engine design attributes gains directly: \texttt{id} adds only
iterative deepening, \texttt{priority-id} adds only the six-class
schedule on top of \texttt{id}, and all other code is shared. Iterative
deepening contributes 60 of the 82 additional decisions over the baseline
(73\,\%); the priority schedule contributes the remaining 22 (27\,\%).

FOLIO is an attribution outlier: \texttt{id} ties \texttt{naive} at 57
because every conversion to \textit{Provable} is offset by another
decision regressing, so all 9 additional FOLIO decisions come from the
priority schedule rather than from iterative deepening.

Roughly 70\,\% of problems remain undecided overall, dominated by
timeouts rather than \textit{Unknown}: the bottleneck is search-space
width, not raw time, and sharper rule-selection would help more than a
larger resource budget.

\section{Conclusion}

Small, principled changes to rule-application order produce disproportionate
improvements in practical solve rate: iterative deepening and the six-class
LK$'$ priority schedule together raise decided problems from 239 to 321
across three diverse datasets without altering the proof calculus.
Key limitations: the fast solve times offered by rust are
non-determinstic due to OS jitter;
no TPTP problem rated above 0.10 is solved, confining the prover to the
easiest tier; direct Rust recursion bounds \texttt{max\_depth} at the
stack limit; and the prover is not competitive with mature systems such
as leanCoP~\cite{otten2003leancop} or Vampire~\cite{kovacs2013vampire}.
Natural successors are lemma sharing (caching closed subgoals) and a
transition to connection-based or saturation-based proof
strategies~\cite{kovacs2013vampire,schulz2002E}.
