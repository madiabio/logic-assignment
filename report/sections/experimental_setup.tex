On TPTP (Figure~\ref{fig:tptp-overview}), \texttt{naive} decides 156,
\texttt{id} 203 ($+30\,\%$), and \texttt{priority-id} 213 ($+37\,\%$);
all gains come from the provable subset, as the four counter-satisfiable
decisions are shared. \texttt{id} and \texttt{priority-id} also solve
shared problems faster on average, consistent with fewer wasted steps in
unclosable branches.

The FOLIO results (Figure~\ref{fig:folio-overview}) reveal a different
pattern: \texttt{naive} and \texttt{id} both decide 57 problems but with
shifted composition (\texttt{naive} 34/23 \textit{Provable}/\textit{NotProvable};
\texttt{id} 40/17). Iterative deepening converts 11 naive-timeouts to
\textit{Provable} but offsets this by causing 5 \textit{Provable}
decisions to time out and 6 \textit{NotProvable} decisions to become
\textit{Unknown}/\textit{Timeout}. \texttt{priority-id} resolves the
trade-off, deciding 66 problems (38/28) by converting 11 of \texttt{id}'s
\textit{Unknown}/\textit{Timeout} outcomes to \textit{NotProvable} and
reducing \textit{Unknown} from 25 to 10.

On the synthetic dataset (Figure~\ref{fig:ai-overview}), \texttt{naive}
decides 26, \texttt{id} 39 ($+50\,\%$), and \texttt{priority-id} 42
($+62\,\%$). Gains concentrate in the medium and hard tiers
(Table~\ref{tab:synthetic-tier}, Appendix~\ref{app:tables}); no engine
decides any expert-tier problem, and all 43 transitivity-chain problems
time out under every engine. Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic-depth}
(Appendix~\ref{app:figures}) confirms \texttt{naive}'s solve rate
collapses beyond depth~4 while \texttt{id} and \texttt{priority-id}
maintain non-zero rates into the hard tier: the signature of~(F2).
