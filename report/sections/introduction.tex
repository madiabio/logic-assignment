\section{Introduction}

Automated theorem proving (ATP) for first-order logic (FOL) is a mature field
with industrial-strength tools such as
Vampire~\cite{kovacs2013vampire}, E~\cite{schulz2002E}, and the connection-based
leanCoP~\cite{otten2003leancop}. These provers are heavily engineered and
implement sophisticated techniques (resolution, superposition, 3indexing,
clause selection) that are far removed from the textbook sequent calculus
introduced by Gentzen~\cite{gentzen1935untersuchungen}. The pedagogical
LK$'$ calculus presented in Hou~\cite{hou2021fundamentals} sits at the other
end of the spectrum: rules manipulate sequents directly, and a backward proof
search procedure (Algorithm~2 in that text) can be stated in twenty lines of
pseudocode. The same simplicity that makes LK$'$ accessible, however, makes
the na\"ive search strategy fragile: nothing prevents the prover from
choosing an unhelpful rule order or descending into unbounded
branching.

This paper revisits Algorithm~2 from a practical standpoint. We give a
faithful Rust implementation of the baseline, identify two failure modes,
and propose two concrete algorithmic improvements that address them: a
six-class \emph{priority schedule} that refines the ``\textbf{else if}''
chain of Algorithm~2 (Sect.~\ref{sec:approach}), and an
\emph{iterative-deepening} envelope around the depth-first
search~\cite{korf1985iterative}. An empirical evaluation on a 737-problem
provable subset of TPTP shows that the combined prover closes 37.5\,\%
more problems than the baseline within a one-second budget
(Sect.~\ref{sec:implementation}).
