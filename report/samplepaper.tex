\subsection{Failure modes}
We identify two structural weaknesses of Algorithm~\ref{alg:naive}.
\textbf{(F1) Eager eigenconstants.} Group~2 lumps the eigenvariable
quantifier rules ($\forall R$, $\exists L$) with non-branching
propositional rules, so the prover commits to a fresh eigenconstant
even when a branching propositional decomposition would have closed the
branch by identity. Each eigenconstant enlarges the symbol set, which
enlarges the candidate set for subsequent $\forall L$/$\exists R$
instantiations.
\textbf{(F2) Unbounded reusable instantiation.} Because $\forall L$ and
$\exists R$ retain the original quantified formula on the sequent,
groups~4 and 5 of Algorithm~\ref{alg:naive} permit an arbitrary number
of distinct witness terms to be tried before any sibling branch is
revisited. A na\"ive depth-first loop therefore commits to deep
instantiation chains before backtracking to easier alternatives.

\subsection{Proposed Algorithm}

We now present our proposed algorithm, which combines iterative
deepening with a six-class priority schedule to address the failure
modes identified above. The algorithm maintains the core backward
proof-search structure while reordering rule application to delay
expensive quantifier instantiations and avoid deep branching chains.

\subsection{Building block A: iterative deepening}

Our first refinement wraps the depth-first search of
Algorithm~\ref{alg:naive} in an iterative-deepening
envelope~\cite{korf1985iterative}. The prover runs depth-bounded DFS at
limits $d = 1, 2, \ldots, d_{\max}$ and returns the first depth at which
the goal closes. Because backward-search depth is roughly proportional
to the number of rule applications on the longest open branch, this
re-orders the search so that shallow proofs are discovered before any
deep instantiation chain is attempted, mitigating (F2).

\subsection{Building block B: LK$'$ six-class priority schedule}

Our second refinement promotes the five-group ``\textbf{else if}'' chain
of Algorithm~\ref{alg:naive} to a six-class schedule:
(1)~\emph{closing} rules $\mathit{id}, \top R, \bot L$;
(2)~\emph{non-branching propositional} rules $\wedge L, \vee R,
{\rightarrow}R, \neg L, \neg R$;
(3)~\emph{branching propositional} rules $\wedge R, \vee L,
{\rightarrow}L$;
(4)~\emph{eigenvariable quantifier} rules $\forall R, \exists L$;
(5)~\emph{reusable quantifiers with visible terms}: $\forall L, \exists R$
instantiated with a previously unused term already in the sequent;
(6)~\emph{reusable quantifiers, fresh fallback}: $\forall L, \exists R$
instantiated with a freshly generated witness subject to a per-occurrence
budget. The change is twofold. Classes~3 and~4 are reordered: branching
propositional rules are tried before any eigenconstant is introduced,
addressing~(F1). The original group~4/5 of Algorithm~\ref{alg:naive} is
split into classes~5 and~6: the prover always exhausts visible witnesses
before introducing a fresh constant, addressing~(F2). The bounded
fresh-fallback budget guarantees that the search returns
$\textit{Unknown}$ rather than $\textit{NotProvable}$ when only fresh
witnesses remain to be tried, preserving soundness.

\subsection{Combined prover}

Our proposed prover \texttt{priority-id} combines both building blocks:
it uses the six-class priority schedule inside an iterative-deepening
envelope. Two control engines are used for comparison: \texttt{id},
which applies iterative deepening without the priority schedule, and
\texttt{naive}, the unmodified baseline. All three share the
same parser, rule applicators, fresh-name generators, branch-state
bookkeeping, timeout machinery, and result persistence; they differ only
in the search engine that orders rule application.

Figure~\ref{fig:priority-id-engine} shows the iterative-deepening outer
loop retrying \texttt{backwards\_search} from the root at each depth
limit, with the six-class LK$'$ priority scheduler injected in place of

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

The two refinements proposed in this paper address distinct failure modes
of Algorithm~\ref{alg:naive} of \cite{hou2021fundamentals}. Iterative
deepening, the more impactful of the two, recovers proofs that the
depth-first baseline misses by getting stuck in deep
quantifier-instantiation chains: it accounts for 49 of the 55 additional
solves observed in our combined prover. The LK$'$ six-class priority
schedule contributes a smaller but consistent additional gain,
concentrated on problems with many atoms where the visible-terms-first
phase pays for itself by avoiding unnecessary fresh-witness generation.

The remaining 70\,\% of unsolved problems are dominated by timeouts
rather than soundness-preserving \emph{Unknown} outcomes, indicating
that the prover would benefit more from sharper search heuristics than
from a larger resource budget. Promising directions include
unification-based instantiation in the manner of
leanTAP~\cite{beckert1995leantap}, term ordering for $\forall L$/$\exists R$
witness selection, and lemma sharing across iterative-deepening
iterations to amortise the re-traversal overhead. Extending the
evaluation to the unprovable problem class would also test the dual
question of how quickly the prover can saturate a non-theorem and report
\emph{NotProvable} rather than time out.

The principal lesson of this study is that small, principled changes to
the rule-application order of a textbook sequent calculus can produce
disproportionate improvements in practical solve rate, even before any
of the heavyweight machinery (resolution, superposition, free-variable
unification) of mature first-order provers is brought to bear.
